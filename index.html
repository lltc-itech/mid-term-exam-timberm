The Birth of the World Wide Web

Sir Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web in 1989.

He was born in 1955 in London, England. His parents were computer scientists who worked on early computers. Eventually, he went on to study at Oxford and earned a degree in physics.

Later, he worked as an independent contractor at CERN, which a particle physics laboratory in Geneva, Switzerland.

While at CERN, in 1980, he proposed his hypertext project as a way to share information among researchers. This was the first iteration of what we now call the World Wide Web.

He later left and finally returned to CERN in 1989, where he brought together a few different ideas, such as the domain name system, and the TCP protocol, to build a new kind of way to navigate the Internet. Thus the World Wide Web was born.

Berners-Lee has spent much of the rest of his career working to pioneer and refine policy that would govern how the Web works and how it would be regulated.
